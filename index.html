This paper examines the integration of artificial intelligence into public administration — from welfare algorithms to automated decision-making systems — analysing accountability deficits, erosion of due process, and pathways to rights-respecting governance. Working Paper No. 1

Automating the State: Promise, Peril, and the Politics of AI in Public Administration

AuthorSwastik Sharma
Year2026
SeriesSVADHINA Working Papers
AreaAI Governance · Public Administration
Abstract

Governments around the world are turning to artificial intelligence to reform how they deliver services, make decisions, and govern populations. This paper offers a systematic analysis of AI integration in public administration — examining both the transformative potential and the structural risks of algorithmic governance. Drawing on case studies including the Netherlands Kinderopvangtoeslag (Child Benefit) scandal and Australia's Robodebt scheme, it demonstrates how automated decision-making can systematically harm citizens when accountability mechanisms fail to keep pace with technological change. The paper analyses the political economy of AI adoption in public institutions, arguing that opacity, speed, and scale create accountability vacuums incompatible with democratic governance. It concludes by proposing an integrated framework covering transparency requirements, human oversight mandates, and redress mechanisms for rights-respecting AI in democratic states.

About SVADHINA

स्वाधीन Svādhīna — Sanskrit · sva (self) + adhīna (under authority of)

Meaning self-governed, free, or autonomous — one who is under their own authority. Applied here: the pursuit of self-determining, rights-grounded governance in the age of artificial intelligence.

The Initiative

Svadhina is an independent research initiative dedicated to studying the intersection of artificial intelligence, governance, and public policy. The initiative focuses on how emerging technologies reshape state capacity, public administration, and democratic institutions.

We produce rigorous, evidence-based research that bridges academic scholarship and practical policy. Our work is oriented toward India and South Asia while engaging with comparative international governance and regulatory developments.

Svadhina is non-partisan and maintains full editorial independence. We do not accept funding that would compromise the independence of our research or editorial positions.

Why This Research Matters

Governments worldwide are adopting AI systems at speed — for welfare delivery, law enforcement, immigration, taxation, and public health. These decisions carry profound consequences for citizens, institutions, and the future of democratic accountability.

Yet the policy frameworks, legal instruments, and institutional capacities needed to govern AI in the public sector remain underdeveloped. Svadhina exists to help close that gap through independent, rigorous scholarship.
